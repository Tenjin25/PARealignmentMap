# Pennsylvania Political Realignment Map (2000-2024)

An interactive web-based visualization exploring Pennsylvania's transformation from a reliably Democratic state to America's most critical swing state. This project analyzes 24 years of election data across all 67 Pennsylvania counties, tracking how voting patterns have shifted and what these changes mean for presidential politics.

## üó∫Ô∏è Overview

Pennsylvania has undergone a dramatic political realignment over the past two decades. This visualization provides an interactive framework to explore that transformation at the county level, revealing:

- **Working-class realignment**: Counties like Lackawanna (Biden's hometown) and Luzerne flipped from Democratic strongholds to Republican leans
- **Suburban shifts**: Philadelphia collar counties (Montgomery, Delaware, Chester, Bucks) transformed from Republican to Democratic
- **Urban Democratic firewall**: Philadelphia and Allegheny (Pittsburgh) counties generate 500,000+ Democratic votes, but face overwhelming Republican margins in rural PA
- **The 2024 result**: Trump won Pennsylvania 50.50% to 48.70%, reestablishing Republican presidential dominance

## üìä Features

- **Interactive Mapbox visualization** showing county-level election results with color-coded competitiveness categories
- **Contest selector** to compare results across presidential, Senate, gubernatorial, and other statewide races
- **Statewide temperature bar** displaying aggregate margins and vote production
- **County profiles** providing historical context and demographic analysis
- **Completeness**: 2000-2024 data across all 67 Pennsylvania counties

### Political Categories

Results are categorized by margin of victory:

- **Annihilation** (40%+): Overwhelming one-party dominance
- **Dominant** (30-39.99%): Strong one-party state
- **Stronghold** (20-29.99%): Likely one-party
- **Safe** (10-19.99%): Probable one-party
- **Likely** (5.50-9.99%): Lean toward one party
- **Lean** (1-5.49%): Slight preference for one party
- **Tilt** (0.50-0.99%): Minimal margin
- **Tossup** (<0.50%): Essentially tied

## üìà Key Findings

### Lackawanna County: Biden's Hometown and the Working-Class Realignment
Joe Biden's childhood home in Scranton (Lackawanna County) exemplifies the working-class realignment:
- 2008: D+25.99% (Obama's peak in this labor county)
- 2016: D+3.48% (Trump: R+22.60% swing in one cycle)
- 2020: D+8.37% (Biden recovers his hometown coalition)
- 2024: D+2.79% (Harris: barely holds on, margins too narrow to save Senate races)

**The story**: Lackawanna swung 22.6 points toward Trump in 2016‚Äîone of the largest single-cycle swings in the state. Biden partially recovered the county in 2020, but the gains were insufficient. The working-class defection from Democratic candidates was so severe that it flipped neighboring Luzerne County to Trump and narrowly lost Senate races in 2024 despite Harris's narrow margin in Lackawanna itself.


### Philadelphia: Democratic Firewall
Philadelphia County (1.6M residents) is Democrats' most valuable asset:
- Delivers 400,000+ Democratic vote margins
- Voting 75-85% Democratic across expanding urban professional base
- Growing Democratic margins each cycle (D+33% in 2016 ‚Üí D+40% in 2024)

### Bob Casey's Historic Defeat: The Working-Class Realignment Goes National

Senator Bob Casey Jr.'s 2024 loss to Republican Dave McCormick represents the culmination of the working-class realignment that has reshaped Pennsylvania politics since 2016.

#### The Numbers

**2018 (Casey's safe re-election) vs. 2024 (Casey's historic defeat):**
- **Luzerne County** (anthracite, working-class): Casey won D+7.00% in 2018 ‚Üí Lost R+18.00% in 2024 (-25.00% swing)
- **Lackawanna County** (Biden's hometown): Casey won D+10%+ in 2018 ‚Üí Won by only D+2.80% in 2024 (swing of -7-8%)
- **Beaver County** (steel country): Already flipped Republican but margins expanded further R+21%
- **Greene County** (coal): Decisively Republican R+45.70%

#### Why Casey Lost Despite Winning Philadelphia

McCormick won by just R+0.22% (48.82%-48.60%, 15,115 votes), the tightest Senate race. Casey's math failed because:

1. **Urban gains insufficient**: Philadelphia margins grew D+40%+, adding ~50,000 votes for Democrats
2. **Suburban slippage**: Bucks County, once a Republican stronghold, went Democratic but not by enough; Montgomery County's shift smaller than 2022
3. **Rural/working-class avalanche**: In 60+ rural/exurban counties, Republicans ran up historic margins (60-75%), offsetting Philadelphia
4. **The Luzerne catastrophe**: Losing Luzerne by 18% in a Senate race was unprecedented; the county represents Pennsylvania's union, working-class Democratic base

#### What Made 2024 Different

Casey had survived the 2016 Trump wave, winning re-election in 2018 during a midterm. But 2024 was different:

- **Turnout failure**: Democrats didn't mobilize their urban base like they had in 2020
- **Working-class rejection**: Harris's campaign failed to connect with non-college voters; McCormick, a wealthy businessman, positioned himself as an outsider
- **Abortion localization failed**: Casey's pro-labor, anti-abortion positioning couldn't overcome economic messaging in working-class regions
- **The incumbent penalty**: After Biden's unpopular tenure and Harris's last-minute candidacy, anti-incumbent sentiment hurt Casey
- **Congressional coattails**: Voters who picked Trump for president also picked Republicans in Senate races (less ticket-splitting than 2020)

#### Luzerne County: The Bellwether of Working-Class Flight

Luzerne exemplifies the Democratic collapse among working-class voters:

| Election | Dem Candidate | Republican | Margin |
|----------|---------------|-----------|--------|
| **2008** | Obama | McCain | D+8.41% |
| **2012** | Obama | Romney | D+4.81% |
| **2016** | Clinton | Trump | R+19.44% (18-point swing) |
| **2020** | Biden | Trump | R+14.39% (Trump consolidation) |
| **2022** | Shapiro (Gov) | Mastriano | D+2.00% (Mastriano too extreme) |
| **2024** | Harris (Pres) | Trump | R+19.17% (consolidated Trump support) |

**The pattern**: Once Democrats win a Luzerne-type county (coal/steel/union), they assume the math still holds. But the 2016 realignment was permanent. There's no route back for Democrats until the party rebuilds trust in working-class regions or the working-class coalition shifts again.

#### The Larger Meaning: Pennsylvania's New Politics

Casey's loss shows that Pennsylvania's working-class realignment is now complete and locked in place:

- **Republicans control rural Pennsylvania** (60+ counties) with expanding margins
- **Democrats control urban Pennsylvania** (Philadelphia + Allegheny) with growing margins  
- **Suburbs remain battlegrounds** but not enough to save Democrats when rural margins explode

A future Democratic candidate for Senate would need:
1. Philadelphia: ~500,000 Democratic votes (growing, achievable)
2. Suburbs: Solid 3-5% margins in Bucks, Montgomery, Delaware, Chester
3. **One major county flip back**: Either Luzerne or another anthracite/steel county must return to Democratic lean
4. **Turnout in swing counties**: Close margins in Dauphin, Lancaster, Erie, Centre

Without all four, Republicans will dominate Senate elections in Pennsylvania.

**Bob Casey's defeat (R+0.22%, 48.82%-48.60%, McCormick 3,399,295 vs Casey 3,384,180) was the tightest Senate race, showing how razor-thin the Republican advantage was‚Äîbut the working-class realignment made it insurmountable for a lifetime incumbent who had always held labor support.**

#### Comparing McCormick to Barletta: Two Different Outsiders

Dave McCormick's 2024 victory over Bob Casey was the culmination of what Lou Barletta's 2018 campaign against Casey attempted but failed. Both were wealthy businessmen outsiders, but their contexts‚Äîand outcomes‚Äîwere fundamentally different.

**Lou Barletta (2018): The Builder Who Lost by Double Digits**

Barletta was Mayor of Hazleton, a small city in working-class Luzerne County known for his hardline immigration stance. He challenged Casey in 2018:

- **Barletta's profile**: Anti-immigration, pro-Trump surrogate, populist appeal, working-class roots
- **The environment**: Trump's 2016 victory had energized Republicans, but 2018 was a midterm backlash year against Trump
- **Barletta's statewide decimation**: Lost to Casey by **D+13.35% statewide** (13-point shellacking to a three-term incumbent)
- **County-level victories** (barely won only 3 counties):
  - Greene County (SW PA): Narrow win
  - Fulton County (SW PA): Narrow win
  - Forest County (NW PA): Narrow win
- **County-level losses in working-class strongholds**:
  - Luzerne (Barletta's own county): Lost by D+7.00% despite being his home base
  - Beaver: Lost by D+2-3% in steel country
  - Northampton: Lost in swing county
  - Berks: Lost in diverse county

**Why Barletta failed**: 2018 was a Democratic midterm wave. Voters were tired of Trump, and Barletta was too closely identified with Trump. Working-class voters who supported Trump in 2016 weren't yet ready to make it permanent across the board‚Äîthey still voted Democratic in statewide races. Barletta's narrow victories in only 3 rural PA counties showed the limits of Trump-focused populism in a midterm backlash.

---

**Dave McCormick (2024): The Tech Billionaire Who Won**

McCormick was a hedge fund manager, Gulf War veteran, and outsider to elected politics. He challenged Casey in 2024:

- **McCormick's profile**: Wealthy but not flamboyant, veteran, anti-China hawk, economic populist messaging, no political past
- **The environment**: Biden's unpopular presidency, Harris's last-minute candidacy, economic backlash, working-class realignment complete and locked in
- **McCormick's Senate margins**: Won by just R+0.22% (48.82%-48.60%, 15,115 votes) statewide (tightest race)
- **County-level results**:
  - Luzerne (Barletta's base): Won for McCormick by 18+ points
  - Beaver: Won decisively (R+20%+)
  - Northampton: Won (swing county tipped)
  - Berks: Won or nearly tied (massive improvement over 2018)

| Factor | Barletta 2018 | McCormick 2024 |

| **Economic message** | Pro-Trump rallies, anti-immigrant | Anti-China economic populism, tariff strategy |
| **Working-class appeal** | Immigration-focused | Cost of living, jobs, manufacturing |

| **Trump factor** | Barletta as Trump lite | McCormick independent, Trump as co-draw |
| **Key counties** | Lost Luzerne D+7% | Won Luzerne R+18% |

#### The Three Counties as Bellwethers

These three counties reveal why McCormick succeeded where Barletta failed:

1. **Northampton (swing county)**: Barletta couldn't win it. McCormick did. The difference: voters needed 6 more years of working-class realignment to solidify, plus Harris couldn't mobilize urban voters like Biden had.

2. **Berks (diverse, Latino growth)**: Barletta lost to Casey. McCormick won or nearly tied. The difference: Latino working-class voters (especially men) rejected Harris's campaign, seeing Republican economic messaging as more relevant than Democratic social progressivism.

3. **Beaver (working-class base)**: Barletta lost narrowly. McCormick won decisively. By 2024, the realignment was complete. Working-class steel country voters weren't hedging their bets anymore.

---

**The Bottom Line**: Barletta was 6 years too early. In 2018, working-class voters still saw Democratic candidates as their party. By 2024, the realignment was permanent enough that even a non-populist businessman (McCormick) could win working-class counties decisively. McCormick won because the working-class realignment was complete, not because he was a superior politician to Barletta‚Äîhe was simply better positioned in 2024.

Counties like Beaver, Greene, Fayette, Washington, and Cambria show massive Republican gains:

Unlike Ohio (now Safe Republican), Pennsylvania stays competitive because:
- Growing/stable metropolitan areas (Philly suburbs, Pittsburgh tech corridor)
- Multiple mid-sized cities (Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Harrisburg, Allentown)
- Increasing college-educated population in suburbs
- More diverse newcomer migration than Rust Belt decline states

## üöÄ Getting Started

### Prerequisites

- Modern web browser with JavaScript enabled (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge)
- Mapbox API token (free at [mapbox.com](https://www.mapbox.com))
- Python 3.8+ (for data processing only, not required to view visualization)

### Installation

1. Clone the repository:
   ```bash
   git clone https://github.com/Tenjin25/PARealignmentMap.git
   cd PARealignmentMap
   ```

2. Set up Mapbox token:
   - Get a free token at [mapbox.com/account/tokens](https://mapbox.com/account/tokens)
   - Open `index.html` and update line ~1320:
     ```javascript
     mapboxToken: 'YOUR_MAPBOX_TOKEN_HERE'
     ```
   - Or set environment variable: `export MAPBOX_TOKEN=pk.eyJ...`

3. Start a local web server:
   ```bash
   # Python 3
   python -m http.server 8000
   
   # Or Node.js (http-server)
   npx http-server
   ```

4. Open browser and navigate to `http://localhost:8000`

### Data Processing (Optional)

To regenerate election data from OpenElections:

```bash
cd scripts
python process_openelections.py
```

This will:
- Process 2000-2024 OpenElections PA data
- Aggregate precinct-level data to county totals
- Generate `data/pa_election_results.json`
- Calculate competitiveness metrics

**Data Strategy:**
- 2000-2014: Uses county-level files directly (complete 67-county coverage)
- 2016-2024: Uses precinct files aggregated to county (ensures geographic completeness)

## üîÑ The Swing State Mechanism: How Pennsylvania Swings

### Three Geographic Coalitions

Pennsylvania's volatility stems from three distinct geographic voting blocs that shift independently:

#### 1. **The Democratic Urban Firewall**
Democratic votes are concentrated in just a few counties:
- **Philadelphia** (1.6M residents): Delivers 400,000+ Democratic margin
- **Allegheny** (Pittsburgh, 1.2M): Delivers 100,000-150,000 Democratic margin
- **Total from 2 counties**: ~500,000 Democratic votes statewide

**Why volatile**: Margin expansion/contraction based on:
  - Turnout in younger, minority, college-educated voters
  - Swing margins upward (2020) or downward (2024) by 5-15%
  - Small percentage shifts create 50,000+ vote changes

#### 2. **The Suburban Battleground (Philadelphia Metro)**
Montgomery, Bucks, Delaware, Chester counties: ~1.5M residents
- Flipped Democratic 2016-2020, remain competitive 2024
- Shifted from Republican strongholds to Democratic-leaning
- Highly educated, professional, cross-pressured voters

**Why volatile**: 
  - Swing 15-20% between cycles
  - Highly responsive to economy, healthcare, social issues
  - College educated increasingly Democratic; non-college Republican

#### 3. **The Republican Exurban/Rural Avalanche**
Small towns, rural, exurban counties across 60+ counties
- Historically Republican but increasing polarization
- Working-class, manufacturing/coal economy
- Trump mobilized non-college voters 2016 onwards

**Why volatile**:
  - Shrinking population but expanding Republican margins
  - Running up 65-75% margins in strongholds
  - Turnout highly responsive to Trump/anti-establishment messaging

### The Math of Swing State Volatility

Republicans need:
- Win the rural avalanche at 60%+ margins ‚úì (consistent 2016-2024)
- Limit Democratic margins in Philadelphia 25-35% margin
- Win suburban counties or come very close

Democrats need:
- Philadelphia 75%+ Democratic (consistent 2000-2024, margins growing)
- Allegheny 60%+ Democratic (consistent, occasionally challenged)
- Win suburbs at 3-5% margin minimum
- Come close in swing counties

**Result**: Margins shift based on:
1. Whether Philadelphia margins expand (2020: D+40%) or contract (2024: D+35%)
2. Whether suburbs go +3-5% Dem or stay flat
3. Whether rural areas reach 65%+ Republican or plateau at 60%

A 2-3% shift in any of these creates a 50,000+ vote swing statewide.

### Key Interpretations

**Pennsylvania Paradox**: Democrats consistently win Senate and gubernatorial races by substantial margins, yet presidential races remain razor-close. This reflects:
- Strength of Democratic urban coalition in local/state races
- Vulnerability to Republican gains in presidential turnout
- Importance of candidate-specific factors (e.g., Shapiro 56%, Harris 48%)

**The Math**: Democrats need:
- Philadelphia 400,000+ Democratic margin
- Allegheny 100,000-150,000 Democratic margin
- Philadelphia suburbs 100,000+ Democratic margin
- **Plus:** wins in swing counties (Dauphin, Centre, Lehigh, Northampton, Erie)

Republicans need:
- Win or come close in swing counties
- Run up 60%+ margins in rural/exurban Pennsylvania (84 counties)
- Limit Democratic margins in urban areas
